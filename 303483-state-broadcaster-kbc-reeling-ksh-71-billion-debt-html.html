State-owned Kenya Broadcasting Corporation (KBC) has sunk deeper into debts, making it more difficult for the broadcaster to execute its growth strategy to catch up with rivals in the private sector.

This was brought to light during a fact-finding tour by the taskforce for improvement of government information and public communications led by David Makali.

The taskforce on Wednesday, April 17, shared it preliminary findings which revealed the state broadcaster had accumulated debts to the tune of KSh 71.3 billion.

According to the report, the biggest percentage of the debt is owed to Japan, followed by staff deductions and then other creditors.

The Makali-led taskforce report indicated the staff deductions stood at KSh 3,024,019, 340, creditors KSh 1,170,956,802 and the Japanese loan KSh 71,302,729,000 including principal and accrued interest as of April 2019.

The cash-strapped public broadcaster's loan with Japan can be traced back to 1989 when it entered into a contract with Japan Telecommunications Engineering Consultancy Service to purchase equipment to expand its national medium wave frequency radio broadcasting network.

The broadcaster's newly appointed boss, Bilal Naim, noted the massive debts had made it difficult for the company to attract and retain talent which is a fundamental part of its growth strategy.

The managing director, however, assured Kenyans things will be fine as he had already put in place a number of measures to help KBC recover and reposition itself as the premier public broadcaster.

The measures being taken included clearing the debts, changing business model and improving signal strength across the country to ensure quality service delivery.

An earlier report by TUKO.co.ke had exposed the grave salary crisis at KBC after which the corporation's top brass was forced to swing into action in what was seemed as a desperate move to save face.

Employees had gone for months without receiving a penny in their bank accounts.

After a whopping seven months of hard work and no pay spanning between November 2017 and June 2018, some 77 employees received a temporary reprieve after the media house disbursed part of their dues.
